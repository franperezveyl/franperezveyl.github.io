Curriculum Vitae
I am a macroeconomist working primarily on labor economics. My research studies how households, workers, and firms adjust their behavior in response to constraints related to technology, labor market regulation, care responsibilities, and social norms. I am interested in understanding how they shape time allocation, occupational choices, and labor demand. I use rich microdata and a combination of causal inference and structural methods.

Working Papers

Sibling Arrival, Family Composition, and the Schooling Trajectories of Firstborns
with Valentina Paredes — Draft coming soon
Family characteristics shape educational outcomes as younger siblings can increase caregiving demands and reduce parental resources. Using Chilean administrative data and a student fixed-effects design, we analyze how younger siblings affect firstborns' grades, attendance, and dropout rates from birth through adolescence. While the arrival of a younger sibling generally lowers grades, it simultaneously improves school attendance and reduces school dropout. Crucially, the negative impacts on achievement are concentrated in households undergoing restructuring, whereas effects are significantly attenuated or reversed for full-siblings who share both biological parents. These findings suggest that the "sibling effect" is closely tied to household stability and gendered caregiving roles, and provide a nuanced view of how family complexity shapes human capital accumulation in emerging economies.
Learning or Screening? Evaluative Feedback and Venture Performance in Entrepreneurial Ecosystems
with Rodrigo Wagner — Draft coming soon
Organizations that evaluate new ventures — accelerators, corporate venture arms, and investor syndicates — routinely generate expert assessments that shape selection decisions. Whether sharing these assessments with entrepreneurs creates value or merely accelerates sorting is a central question for the strategic design of entrepreneurial ecosystems. We provide causal evidence using a randomized field experiment in Start-Up Chile, a global startup accelerator. Half of 88 admitted ventures were randomly assigned to receive written feedback from independent expert judges. We find that feedback increases the likelihood of securing external funding by 7 percentage points and the amount raised by approximately \$15,000 on average, and raises the likelihood of survival years after the program. Effects are concentrated in the upper tail and stronger when feedback is more extensive. Overall, the results point to a value-enhancing role of feedback rather than a pure screening mechanism.
Regulating Flexible Labor Contracts: Substitution and Allocative Efficiency
with Alejandro Micco
Regulations governing flexible labor contracts shape firms’ adjustment margins and the allocation of labor across heterogeneous producers. We study the aggregate consequences of such regulation using Chile’s 2006 reform of temporary agency work. Combining plant-level data with a dynamic industry equilibrium model featuring non-convex firing costs and imperfect substitution between regular and agency workers, we show that the reform shut down a key adjustment margin by raising both fixed and variable costs of agency labor. Aggregate productivity declines, even though average firm-level productivity remains unchanged, reflecting increased dynamic misallocation rather than within-firm efficiency losses. Output prices rise as higher labor costs and impaired reallocation contribute in roughly equal measure, with their relative importance governed by the elasticity of substitution across worker types. Our results highlight that regulating flexible employment arrangements can generate sizable productivity losses by weakening firms’ dynamic adjustment margins, effects that are not captured by static or reduced-form approaches.
