Curriculum Vitae
I am a macroeconomist working primarily on labor economics. My research explores the aggregate implications of frictional labor markets and other market imperfections. I currently investigate the gendered division of household work and its implications for the labor market, and the impact of automation on employment trends and the gender wage gap.

Research

Working Papers

Sibling Arrival, Family Composition, and the Schooling Trajectories of Firstborns
with Valentina Paredes — Draft coming soon
Family characteristics shape educational outcomes as younger siblings can increase caregiving demands and reduce parental resources. Using Chilean administrative data and a student fixed-effects design, we analyze how younger siblings affect firstborns' grades, attendance, and dropout rates from birth through adolescence. While the arrival of a younger sibling generally lowers grades, it simultaneously improves school attendance and reduces school dropout. Crucially, the negative impacts on achievement are concentrated in households undergoing restructuring, whereas effects are significantly attenuated or reversed for full-siblings who share both biological parents. These findings suggest that the "sibling effect" is closely tied to household stability and gendered caregiving roles.
Learning or Screening? Experimental Evidence on Feedback in a Startup Accelerator
with Rodrigo Wagner — Draft coming soon
Evaluative feedback is a common feature of startup accelerators and venture competitions, yet its effects are theoretically ambiguous. We address this challenge using a randomized field experiment in Start-Up Chile. Among 88 admitted ventures, half were randomly assigned to receive written feedback on their business plans from independent expert judges. We find that feedback increases the likelihood of securing external funding by 7 percentage points and raises the amount raised by approximately $15,000 on average, and raises the likelihood of survival several years after the program. The results point to a value-enhancing role of feedback rather than a pure screening mechanism.
Regulating Flexible Labor Contracts: Substitution and Allocative Efficiency
with Alejandro Micco
Regulations governing flexible labor contracts alter firms' adjustment margins and the allocation of labor across heterogeneous producers. Using Chile's 2006 labor reform, which increased the cost of temporary agency workers, as a policy shock, we combine plant-level data with a dynamic industry equilibrium model featuring imperfect substitution between regular and temporary agency workers. The model predicts higher output prices, driven roughly equally by increased labor misallocation and by the direct increase in labor costs. The results highlight how regulating flexible employment arrangements reshapes aggregate outcomes through both cost and allocation channels.
